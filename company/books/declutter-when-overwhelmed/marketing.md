## 1. Blurb

You don't have a mess problem. You have a decision problem — and nobody ever told you that.

Every object in a cluttered room is actually three or four unmade decisions stacked on top of each other: keep or not, where it goes, how you feel about it, whether you can defend the choice. Your brain has a limited daily supply of good decision-making, and decluttering burns through it faster than almost anything else you do — which is why you can start strong on a Saturday morning and freeze completely by 11 a.m.

This book doesn't give you more willpower tips. It gives you a system built to need less willpower: pre-made keep/toss rules you decide once instead of a thousand times, a four-zone sort that touches each object exactly once, an attack order that saves the hardest categories for last, a separate slower process for sentimental items, and 90-second sessions for the days you have nothing left. If you've tried and stalled before, this is why — and this is the fix.

## 3. Social Captions

**1. Surprising insight**
Picking up a random object during a decluttering session isn't one decision. It's usually four or five stacked on top of each other — what it is, where it goes, how you feel about it, whether you can justify letting it go. That's why a "five-second" item can eat five minutes, and why you freeze on object sixty even though object four felt easy.

**2. Question**
Have you ever started decluttering strong — tossing things fast, feeling great — and then, less than an hour in, just... stopped, standing there holding something totally ordinary, unable to decide? That's not a mood swing. That's a specific, predictable thing happening in your brain. Worth knowing what it actually is before you try again.

**3. Mini-tip**
Next time you declutter, write your keep/toss rules on an index card before you touch anything — real numbers, like "more than 3 identical t-shirts, extras go." When you hit a hard item mid-session, check the card instead of deciding fresh. Matching against a rule costs almost nothing. Deciding from scratch, tired, costs a lot.

**4. Testimonial-style (hypothetical outcome)**
Imagine finally getting through a kitchen junk drawer that's beaten you three separate times — not because you toughed it out longer, but because you sorted by type instead of by object, and made one decision per group instead of twenty separate ones. Same drawer. A fraction of the mental cost. That's the shift this system is built around.

**5. Direct CTA**

It was the number of decisions the task was quietly asking you to make. *Declutter When You're Overwhelmed* lays out the system for making far fewer of them. Grab it and start with a single 90-second session today.
